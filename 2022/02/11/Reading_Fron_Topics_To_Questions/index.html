Reading - From Topics to Questions

Feb 11, 2022

This article provides guidance for beginning researchers on how to choose a research topic, ask a specific question, and find a worthwhile problem to solve.

A research topic can come first from the researcher’s interest while it should also be of interest to others. A topic should be sufficiently narrow, or the research may become a mass of data. But be careful to avoid narrowing the topic to the point where it is difficult to gather information.

Once we have determined a research topic, we can look for specific questions. Gathering information about the history, composition structure, and categories of the topic can help us raise specific questions. Before proceeding to the next step, the questions should be evaluated to ensure the answers are significant, and to confirm that the questions to be explored are also meaningful to other researchers or related individuals.

Next, we will turn the problem into a problem worth solving. Throughout this step, the article emphasizes that the answer to a problem should helps understanding something more significant than the answer itself or avoid undesired cost in practice. In addition, talking to other researchers, looking for academic resources in libraries and on the Internet are also good ways to develop questions and problems.

Question:
When referring to the meaning of “conceptual problems,” the article explains that solving such problems is a sure way to understand something else even more significant. However, in our experience and in the articles we read about art research in the last two weeks, the research in the design field is by case, addressing more specific problems. So would it be closer to the practical problem? And if we already consider design as a means of solving problems, what significant further insights can we get from researching on it?
