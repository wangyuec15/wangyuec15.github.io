Reading - From Problems to Sources

Feb 17, 2022

Summary:

Three kinds of sources:

  • Primary sources:“original” materials that provide the “raw data” or evidence for developing, testing and justify research hypothesis or claim
  • Secondary sources: books, articles, or reports that are based on primary sources and are intended for scholarly or professional audiences
  • Tertiary Sources: books and articles that synthesize and report on secondary sources for general readers

When making scholarly argument, build them on secondary sources rather than tertiary sources.

How to find sources in the library:

  • Ask librarian to help refine search parameters and get the right tools for specific research question
  • Consult reference works to know how to find sources
  • Explore online databases of academic libraries and public libraries

When finding sources on the Internet:

  • Maintain a healthy skepticism: not everything is perfectly reliable as library’s catalogs and databases
  • We can explore potential keywords to use in a more systematic search, remind ourselves of dates or facts, locate the authors of sources whom we might wish to contact.

How to evaluate sources for relevance:

  • For books: skim where key words occur, first and last paragraphs in chapters, prologues, introductions, summary chapters, and so on. Check the bibliography for titles relevant to your topic.
  • For articles: Read the abstract, introduction and conclusion, first and last several paragraphs, and section headings. Also, check the bibliography for titles relevant to your topic

How to evaluate sources for reliability:

  • A reliable source would: published or posted online by a reputable press, peer-reviewed, cite a reputable scholar, and up-to-date.
  • A reliable book should also have notes and a bibliography and been well reviewed
  • A reliable website should also include bibliographical data and approach its topic judiciously
  • A frequently cited source is more likely influential

You can also look beyond the standard kinds of references relevant to your question, and interview people in relevant business, government, or civic organizations

Reflection:

Searching for sources is important in the starting stage of research, and in this article I learned how to search for sources that are most relevant to the research question in various ways, which sources to cite when making an argument, and how to verify the reliability of the sources. I think what the authors emphasize the most, and what we should be most careful about from the preparation phase of our research, is reliability. For the various search methods mentioned in the article are built on it to ensure that the sources are reviewed and certified by professionals in the context of academic research and are qualified to become the reference of future research. I think I should also hold my research to the standard of a reliable source.

Question:

A funny thought: If I get a snip of code as an appendix of an academic article, should I consider it as a primary source (literally raw data) or secondary source (some other researcher’s work)?
In the previous article, it was mentioned that if the amount of sources available to us is troubling us, it is probably because there is something wrong with the scope or orientation of our question. How do you think the sources obtained can guide us in making adjustments to our research questions?
