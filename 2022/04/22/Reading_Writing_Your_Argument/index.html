Reading - Writing Your Argument

Apr 22, 2022

Planning and drafting

Points to avoid

  • Do not organize report as “a narrative of your thinking”(P177): should provide the readers the clear main point of the results of the research, rather than what you are thinking.
  • Do not assemble your report as a patchwork of your sources(P178): should provide analysis of sources rather than their summary.
  • Do not “map your report directly on to the language of your assignment”(p179): you want to mention conceptual topics or definitions instead of listing a series of issues to cover.

Drafting introduction

It is helpful to have a “working introduction” at the beginning of writing before you have the final one for your readers.

  1. Have a brief summary for only the sources that are intended to correct, modify or expand on.
  2. Rephrase questions as a “statement about a flaw or gap that you see in them”(P179)
  3. Mention the importance to solve the question by answering “So what if we don’t find out?”(P180)
  4. State or promise the answer to my question. Choose whether it is better to state the main claim in the beginning or at the final to create a “controlling relationship”(P180) with reader.
    • Keep the key concepts in mind when drafting the whole report
    • For the report body, you should concisely locate the problem in a background and define terms, then choose a suitable order for the major sections of the report.
    • Then in each report section, highlight the “key terms” to distinguish it, and organize the “evidence, acknowledgments, warrants, and summaries”(P184)
    • In terms of conclusion, state the point and its significance again.
  • A exploratory draft report may help discover ideas, but if deadline is near it is better to have a clearer plan when drafting. Write in a pace that makes yourself feel most comfortable.
  • Key words helps with storyboard to keep the draft “on track”(P187), so having headings in draft helps structure the report.
  • Decide how to use information. Summarize for less important sources, paraphrase those you can state “more clearly or concisely or when your argument depends on”(P188), and quote for evidence, authority strikingly origin “backs up your reasons” or that you disagree with.(P189)
  • For direct quotations, you can have the shorter ones in-line with quotation marks and the longer ones in block. Identify the author, “modify a quotation so long as don’t change its meaning, delete with three dots and change with square brackets”(P190)
  • To stress a source’s relevance to your reasons, you should state clear “what you want your readers to get out of it” explaining the evidence.

Avoid plagiarism:

  • “Cite the Source of Every Quotation, Paraphrase, or Summary”(P192). Keep clear track of which words are borrowed from source. Include the citation “as soon as you add a quotation”
  • “Signal Every Quotation, Even When You Cite Its Source”(P193) for reader to know the exactly word from others. Better to put too much quotation marks than missing them.
  • Avoid “Paraphrase Too Closely”(P194). Paraphrase later after reading the source may help.Usually cite “a Source for Ideas Not Your Own”(P195), mainly those “associated with a specific person” and too new to become a field’s common knowledge.
  • Don’t “Plead Ignorance, Misunderstanding, or Innocent Intentions”(P195). Must site a source even if it’s “free and publicly available”citations protect the researchers from “charge of plagiarism”, help readers to verify the evidence and start their research, and “honor the sources”(P196)

Communicating Evidence Visually

  • For complex data, tables, charts and line graphs can present them directly. When designing visualization, you want to “keep titles and legends short but descriptive”(P216), insert inner informations to support your point, and include how to interpret it(P218)
  • Keep the graphics simple to stress how they relevant to your point.
  • Be not only “clear and accurate, but honest” (P226). Avoid implying different messages, false correlations or “encourages readers to misjudge values”(P228)

Reflection:

What inspired me the most in this reading was the part about avoiding plagiarism. When we make designs, there are sometimes gray areas that allow for references and homages. But when conducting academic research, we must take each source seriously and distinguish between our own and others’ contributions, in order to have a positive academic environment and to grant the researcher the recognition and respect he or she deserves. The approach mentioned in this chapter is also well worth keeping in mind when writing papers.

Question:

When writing a draft report, we may not have collected enough points to make a strong structure of storyboard, what can we do at this stage for making this draft most helpful for a clearer one?
