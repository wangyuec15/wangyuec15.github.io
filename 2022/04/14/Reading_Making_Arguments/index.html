Reading - Making Arguments

Apr 14, 2022

Summary:
Making good arguments:

  • build arguments out of the answers to five questions (P109)
    1. What is my claim?
    2. What reasons support my claim?
    3. What evidence supports my reasons?
    4. Do I acknowledge alternatives / complications / objections, and how do I respond?
    5. What warrant makes my reasons relevant to my claim?
  • claim should be supported by reasons and evidence. A reason, is a “statement that gives your readers cause to accept your claim”(P109). Evidence is “what you and your readers can see, touch, taste, smell, or hear”(p111) on which you base your reasons.
  • imagine readers’ questions, alternatives, and objections and make acknowledgment/responses to them.
  • warrants is needed to show “how a reason is relevant to a claim” (p112). You must “offer a general principle that justifies relating your particular reason to your particular claim”(P114)
  • building a complex argument: respond to several alternatives and objections; support elements of argument by treating them as subclaim; explain “background, definitions, explanations of issues that readers might not understand” (p117)

Making claims

  • determine the kind of claim to make: if conceptual, be explicit about what action you want them to take; if practical, “explains what causes the problem” and “explains how doing what you propose will fix it”(p121).
  • four sub-arguments to explain:
    1. Why your solution is feasible; how it can be implemented with reasonable time and effort.
    2. Why it will cost less to implement than the cost of the problem.
    3. Why it will not create a bigger problem than the one it solves.
    4. Why it is cheaper or faster than alternative solutions—a claim often difficult to support.
  • make claim specific: “be only as specific as your readers need” and “include only those concepts that you develop as themes in your argument”(p122) Introduce claim logic with a qualifying clause or conclude it with a reason clause.
  • make claim significant: significance is a quality readers measure by “how much it asks them to change what they think”(p124). Significant research offer not only new data but uses them to “settle what seems puzzling, inconsistent, or otherwise problematical” or “upset what seemed long settled”(p125)

Assembling Reasons and Evidence

  • plan argument by ordering reasons, storyboard may be helpful to make sure that order makes consecutive sense.
  • make sure you have sufficient evidence to support each reason. To count as evidence, “a statement must report a shared, public fact”(p131)
  • evidence is different from the report of evidence: data taken from a source “have invariably been shaped by that source”, and would be manipulated again by “putting them in a new context”(p134). So make sure the data can be trusted by the readers.
  • reliable evidence should be: “sufficient and representative, reported accurately and precisely” and “taken from an authoritative source”(p136) and stated precisely according to the standard of their fields.
  • provide evidence that be sufficient to prove a claim, be “representative of the full range of variation in what’s available”(p138)

Acknowledgments and Responses

  • imagine and respond to readers’ predictable questions and objections. There are intrinsic doubt about your claim reasons and evidence, and extrinsic questions about alternative ways of framing the problem, evidence or others’ work.
  • question your own argument by questioning the problem, solution and support. Find out where your argument “looks weak but is not”, “defuse that concern by acknowledging and responding to it”(p141).
  • find alternatives: how others thought about your question or topic, source takes different approaches or different aspects of problem.
  • decide what to acknowledge: acknowledge alternatives and “use the response to reinforce your point”, use close alternative to “reiterate the virtues of your solution”(p144), acknowledge alternatives that may particularly appeal to your readers without dismissive.
  • acknowledge to questions you can’t answer: Candidly acknowledge the problem and respond that “the rest of your argument more than balances the flaw”, “more research will show a way around this flaw” or “your argument offers important insight into the question and suggests what a better answer would need with the flaw”(p145)
  • frame your responses as subordinate arguments: explain the basis of response using additional reasons and evidence.

Question:

  1. Sometimes our evidence involves factors such as design experience or design performance, how can we express it in a way that will convince the reader?

  2. How to make sure we offer important insight into the question when there is question we cannot answer?
